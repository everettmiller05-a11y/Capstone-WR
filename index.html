01 · Background

Finding My Place
in the Music Industry

When I started at Warner Records, I didn't have the traditional music industry background. My experience was in AI, data, startups, and business, but music had always been something I genuinely loved.

This internship showed me that there isn't one path into the industry. Instead of trying to fit a mold, I learned how to bring my own skills into A&R and contribute in a meaningful way.

A different kind of curiosity

Coming from AI, data, and UX meant I asked different questions. Sometimes that was exactly what the conversation needed.

Data Science, applied to music

My DS & AI background trained me to spot patterns and ask why something is working, not just whether it is.

Music was always genuine

It wasn't something I added to my resume for this role. Music has been a constant part of my life, and that came through in the work.

02 · Experience

My time at
Warner Records

As an A&R Research Intern, I focused on how A&R teams find artists, decide who's worth betting on, and what that decision actually looks like up close. I also found a way to bring my technical background directly into the work.

Warner Records · Los Angeles

Artist Research

Spent time finding and researching emerging artists by looking at streaming data, social media, release history, and overall momentum. The biggest thing I learned was that it's less about one viral moment and more about spotting artists who are consistently building something over time.

AI Collaboration & Innovation

I participated in weekly AI meetings where people from across the company shared how they were using AI in their day-to-day work. Hearing different perspectives gave me new ideas for improving my own workflows and reinforced how AI can be used to make creative and operational work more efficient.

AI Workflow Project

Built an AI tool that takes long-form content like interviews and documentaries, finds the most interesting moments, and automatically turns them into social-ready clips for the marketing team. It was a cool opportunity to combine my technical background with what I was learning in the music industry.

Sitting In on A&R Conversations

Getting to observe real A&R discussions was one of the most valuable parts of the internship. I saw firsthand how teams talk about artists, what they listen for, what questions they ask, and how much of the conversation is built on instinct backed by context.

03 · Insights

Turning My Background
Into My Strength

One of the biggest things I learned during my internship is that A&R isn't just about knowing music. It's about being curious, asking good questions, and finding creative ways to understand where an artist is headed.

The hardest part was early on, not knowing the industry's language or how decisions actually get made made it difficult to contribute at first. But sitting with that discomfort taught me more than any shortcut would have.

Coming from technology and data meant I had to earn credibility in a room that runs on taste. Once I stopped trying to translate my background into music-industry terms and just let it inform how I thought, things clicked.

Curiosity Over Credentials

No one cared that I didn't have years of music industry experience. What mattered was showing up prepared, having thoughtful opinions, and being willing to learn from every conversation.

Using My Technical Background

My background in technology didn't disappear when I started working in A&R, it became one of my biggest strengths. It helped me think differently about artist development, data, and how AI can make creative work more efficient.
